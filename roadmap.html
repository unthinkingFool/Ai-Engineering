§1 — The Landscape

"AI Engineer" now means something specific. Build toward the right target.

In 2020, "AI Engineer" and "Machine Learning Engineer" were the same job. By 2026 they've split into two different careers with different stacks, different interview loops, and different hiring markets — because foundation models absorbed the part of the work that used to require training a model from scratch.

A Machine Learning Engineer still builds, trains, and deploys custom models on proprietary data — fraud detection, recommendation ranking, forecasting. The model is the product. An AI Engineer — the role you asked about — builds production software on top of existing foundation models: retrieval pipelines, agents, tool use, evaluation, and the serving layer around them. Roughly two-thirds of the underlying skills overlap (Python, cloud, ML fundamentals); the other third is where the two careers diverge hard.

Why this roadmap targets AI Engineer, specifically

You already mentioned ML algorithms and RAG — you're closer to this track than to classical ML already. This plan builds real depth in classical ML and neural nets too (Phase 2), partly because interviewers test for it regardless of title, and partly because it makes you a credible candidate for ML Engineer postings as a fallback. But the specialization — and the projects — point at AI Engineer roles: the ones built around LLMs, retrieval, agents, and evaluation.

Where your $140K+ target actually sits

It's not a stretch number — it's close to the entry point. Robert Half's national base range for AI/ML engineers starts at $134K before any equity or bonus. Nvidia, Microsoft, Databricks, and Palantir all show entry-to-mid bands that clear $140K comfortably. At true frontier labs (OpenAI, Anthropic), even junior compensation sits far above it — the binding constraint there isn't the offer size, it's getting through an extremely competitive door (see §9). The honest risk to your target isn't the number — it's which door you can realistically get through in month 6, which is why §7 spreads your applications across the whole spectrum in this table, not just the five most famous logos.

A geography note, for whoever's reading this

Every figure above assumes a US-based hire. "Remote" postings at these companies are almost always restricted to specific countries where the company has a legal entity — that fine print matters more than the word "remote" itself. If you're outside the US, your realistic paths to these numbers are, in rough order of likelihood: (1) a regional engineering hub of the same company — Google, Microsoft, and Amazon all run large offices outside the US that hire locally at strong (if geographically-adjusted) bands; (2) a relocation-sponsored offer, which requires a genuinely standout application since sponsorship is an extra cost the employer weighs; (3) a remote-first AI-native company that hires globally by policy — rarer, and rarely at full US comp outside senior levels; (4) using a strong local or regional role as the stepping stone to an internal transfer later. Build the target company list in §7 with this in mind from week one, not in month 6.

§3 — The Build

26 weeks, 6 phases, one checkpoint per week

Calibrated to what you already know — this does not re-teach loops, functions, or what a variable is. Every phase assumes ~30 hrs/week and ends with something deployed, not just something understood. Click a phase to expand it, then click any week for the full breakdown.

PHASE 01
Foundations — compile the toolchain
Weeks 1–4 · ~120 hrs

You already know SQL, C++, Java, and some Python — so this phase doesn't re-teach programming. It sharpens Python specifically for AI/data work, refreshes just enough math to reason about models (not prove theorems), and gets your engineering hygiene — git, Docker, cloud, APIs — to production grade. By week 4 you'll have deployed your first live endpoint.

WK 01Python, the way AI teams actually write it
Learn
  • Idioms C++/Java habits don't give you: comprehensions, generators, context managers, *args/**kwargs, type hints, dataclasses, f-strings
  • Virtual environments and dependency management (venv, pip, requirements.txt)
  • NumPy fundamentals: arrays, broadcasting, vectorization — and why vectorized code beats loops
  • Reading Python tracebacks fluently — the single most time-saving debugging skill
Build
  • Rewrite a small program you'd normally write in Java/C++ (e.g. a CSV parser + aggregator) as idiomatic, vectorized Python/NumPy — benchmark it against a naive loop version and record the speedup
Resources
  • Python official docs — "Classes" & data model chapters · NumPy quickstart · Corey Schafer's Python OOP series (free, YouTube)
WK 02Data wrangling + your first API calls
Learn
  • Pandas: dataframes, groupby, merges/joins, missing-data handling, apply vs. vectorized ops
  • SQL past the basics: window functions, CTEs, reading a query plan — this shows up directly in feature-pipeline interview questions
  • REST APIs in practice: the requests library, auth headers, retries with backoff
  • Your first real calls to the OpenAI and Anthropic APIs: system prompts, temperature, streaming
  • Linear algebra intuition: vectors, matrices, dot product, what an "embedding" geometrically is
Build
  • Pull a public dataset into Pandas, clean it, answer 5 analytical questions using window functions in both SQL and Pandas; separately, a 20-line script that sends the same prompt to OpenAI and Anthropic and diffs the two responses
Resources
  • 3Blue1Brown — "Essence of Linear Algebra" (free) · Mode Analytics SQL tutorial (window functions) · official OpenAI & Anthropic API quickstarts
WK 03Math intuition + your first real ML model
Learn
  • Probability & statistics at an applied level: distributions, expectation/variance, Bayes' theorem, correlation vs. causation
  • The ML mental model: train/validation/test splits, overfitting vs. underfitting, bias-variance tradeoff
  • Evaluation metrics that actually matter: precision/recall/F1, ROC-AUC, confusion matrices — and when each one is the wrong choice
  • scikit-learn workflow: pipelines, cross-validation, hyperparameter search
Build
  • Pick a real, messy public dataset — not Titanic or Iris (a churn, fraud, or pricing dataset) — and build a full pipeline: clean → engineer features → train logistic regression + gradient boosting → evaluate properly → write a plain-English paragraph on which metric matters for this business problem and why
Resources
  • StatQuest with Josh Starmer (YouTube) · "Hands-On Machine Learning" by Aurélien Géron, ch. 1–3 · Google's Machine Learning Crash Course
WK 04Ship it: Docker, git, and your first live endpoint
Learn
  • Git past git add .: branches, meaningful commits, pull requests, resolving conflicts
  • Docker fundamentals: images vs. containers, Dockerfile basics, multi-stage builds, why containers matter for reproducibility
  • FastAPI basics: routes, request/response models with Pydantic, automatic docs
  • Pick one cloud (AWS or GCP) — free tier, IAM basics, one compute service
Build
  • Wrap your Week 3 model in a FastAPI endpoint, containerize it with Docker, deploy to a free-tier host (Render, Fly.io, Railway, or AWS/GCP free tier) with a real public URL
Resources
  • Docker's official "Get Started" guide · FastAPI official tutorial · freeCodeCamp Docker crash course (YouTube)

WK 09Working the frontier APIs like an engineer, not a tourist

WK 10Embeddings, vector databases, and your first RAG prototype
Learn
  • Embedding models, similarity metrics (cosine, dot product), vector index structures (HNSW, IVF) conceptually, and when to reach for pgvector (under ~5–10M vectors, simplicity) vs. Qdrant/Weaviate/Pinecone (scale, filtering, managed ops)
Build
  • A naive RAG system: chunk a real document set, embed it, store it in pgvector or Qdrant, retrieve top-k, generate an answer with citations
Resources

WK 11Production-grade retrieval

Flagship Project 1 starts here.

Learn
  • Chunking-strategy tradeoffs (fixed vs. semantic, size/overlap), hybrid search (dense + BM25 via reciprocal rank fusion), cross-encoder reranking, query rewriting / HyDE, multi-turn context handling, graceful "I don't know" instead of hallucinating
Build
  • Upgrade Week 10's prototype into hybrid retrieval + reranking, now against a genuinely large, messy real-world corpus (see §4)
Resources

WK 12Fine-tuning, and knowing when not to
Learn
  • The decision framework interviewers explicitly probe for: RAG for large/changing/citation-needing knowledge; fine-tuning for behavior/format/style; long-context for whole-document reasoning; usually combined in practice. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (LoRA, QLoRA, PEFT), quantization basics
Build
  • Fine-tune a small open model (Llama/Qwen/Mistral small variant) with LoRA on a narrow domain task; compare its outputs against your RAG system and a plain-prompting baseline on the same test questions
Resources
  • Hugging Face PEFT docs · DeepLearning.AI — "Quantization Fundamentals" & "Fine-tuning and RL for LLMs"
PHASE 04
Agents + Evals
Weeks 13–16 · ~120 hrs

Multi-agent orchestration is flagged across multiple 2026 hiring analyses as the single biggest skill gap in AI engineering. This phase builds that, plus the evaluation discipline that separates people who ship demos from people who ship systems.

WK 13Your first agent
Learn
  • The ReAct loop (reason → act → observe → repeat), tool/function definitions, LangGraph fundamentals (state graphs, nodes, edges), why graph-based orchestration beats a hand-rolled while-loop once workflows branch
Build
  • A single agent with 2–3 real tools (web search + calculator + file reader) completing a multi-step task in LangGraph, with visible intermediate reasoning
Resources
  • DeepLearning.AI — "AI Agents in LangGraph" · LangGraph official docs · Hugging Face Agents Course
WK 14Multi-agent systems + MCP
Learn
  • Multi-agent patterns (sequential, supervisor/hierarchical, role-based crews), state handoffs, the Model Context Protocol (MCP) as the standard for connecting agents to tools and data, human-in-the-loop checkpoints for high-stakes actions
Build
  • A 2–3 agent supervisor system (e.g. research agent + writer agent + a supervisor that routes between them) with one MCP-based tool connection and one human-approval gate before any "final" action
Resources
  • LangGraph multi-agent tutorials · CrewAI docs (role-based alternative) · the official Model Context Protocol spec & quickstart
WK 15Evaluation: proving your system actually works
Learn
  • Why "it works on my 3 test prompts" isn't evidence; building a golden dataset (50–100 labeled examples); RAG-specific metrics (faithfulness, answer relevance, context precision/recall via RAGAS); LLM-as-judge patterns and their failure modes; running evals as automated regression tests, not manual spot checks
Build
  • A 50+ question golden dataset for your RAG system, wired into RAGAS and DeepEval for faithfulness/relevance scores, plus a pytest suite that fails the build on regression
Resources
  • RAGAS docs · DeepEval docs (pytest integration) · DeepLearning.AI — "Automated Testing for LLMOps"
WK 16Observability: cost, latency, and what breaks in production

Flagship Project 2 comes together here.

§4 — Flagship Projects

Five projects. Not a chatbot wrapper, not Titanic, not Iris.

Hiring managers report scanning hundreds of portfolios with the same three demos. What actually gets a callback in 2026, per multiple independent engineering-hiring analyses: fewer projects, each carrying real evaluation, real deployment, and a documented failure analysis — not a happy-path notebook. Five strong builds beat twenty shallow ones. Build them in the order the roadmap introduces them.

FLAGSHIP 1 — BUILT IN PHASE 3

Domain Document Intelligence System

A production-grade RAG system over a genuinely large, messy real corpus — not five clean PDFs. Pick a domain with real structure to fight: public tax code + case law, a country's open-data regulatory filings, a large open-source project's full documentation + GitHub issues, or public company annual reports. The point is realistic messiness: multi-column PDFs, tables, cross-references, contradictions between documents.

Must include
  • Hybrid retrieval (dense + BM25) with cross-encoder reranking
  • A justified chunking strategy — written down, not just chosen by default
  • Citation grounding: every answer points to its source passage
  • A 50–100 question golden dataset scored with RAGAS (faithfulness, relevance, context precision)
  • Graceful "I don't know" instead of a confident wrong answer
  • Deployed with a live, clickable demo URL
What it proves to a hiring manager
  • You understand retrieval is the actual hard problem, not the model
  • You measure quality instead of eyeballing three example outputs
  • You can take a system from notebook to something a stranger can click

FLAGSHIP 2 — BUILT IN PHASE 4

Autonomous Multi-Agent Ops System

Multi-agent orchestration is the most commonly cited skill gap in 2026 agent-engineering hiring. Build something that does real, boring, valuable work autonomously: a system that triages incoming GitHub issues on a real open-source repo (classify, draft a response, escalate ambiguous ones), or a document-analysis crew (research agent → analyst agent → report-writer agent, supervised).

Must include
  • A supervisor pattern with real state handoffs between at least 2–3 agents
  • Checkpointing so a long-running task can resume after a crash
  • One human-in-the-loop approval gate before any consequential action
  • An evaluation layer that scores the agent's trajectory, not just its final answer
  • Visible cost controls — a budget the agent can't blow past
What it proves to a hiring manager
  • You can build autonomy with safety rails, not a fragile demo that only works on the happy path
  • You think about what happens when a tool call fails mid-task, not just when it succeeds

FLAGSHIP 3 — BUILT IN PHASE 4/5

LLM Evaluation & Regression-Testing Harness

A meta-project, and arguably the highest-leverage one: build a reusable evaluation framework that gates deploys of Flagships 1 and 2. Most AI engineers ship without this — it's explicitly named as the #1 gap between people who "completed the LLM course" and people who get hired.

Must include
  • Golden datasets per system, version-controlled alongside the code
  • An LLM-as-judge implementation with documented failure modes (when does the judge itself get it wrong?)
  • Integration into GitHub Actions — evals run on every pull request
  • A small dashboard tracking pass-rate over time, so regressions are visible, not discovered by users
What it proves to a hiring manager
  • Production discipline applied to a genuinely hard problem — testing non-deterministic systems
  • You'll say "here's the eval score" in an interview, not "it feels pretty good"

§5 — System Design Playbook

The framework, then the questions to drill it against

System design carries the most weight of any round in a 2026 AI-engineering loop — it's where the strongest candidates show their ceiling and the mediocre ones run out of depth fastest. One recurring line from practitioners doing these interviews now: "evaluation methodology is the new system design" — interviewers increasingly care more about cost, latency, guardrails, and monitoring than about the architecture diagram itself.

A structured way to walk through any design question

01–02Clarify, then frame

Pin down scale, latency, and accuracy requirements before drawing anything. Frame the business problem as an ML/AI problem: what's the objective, what does "success" mean numerically?

03–04Data & features

Where does data come from, how is it validated, batch vs. streaming? For classic ML: offline/online feature stores and how you prevent training-serving skew.

05–06Model & knowledge strategy

For AI-engineer-flavored questions, this is the decision that matters most: RAG for large/changing/citation-needing knowledge, fine-tuning for behavior/format/style, long-context for whole-document reasoning, tools/memory for actions and state — usually combined.

07Serving

Latency budget, dynamic vs. continuous batching, KV-cache reuse, model routing (cheap model for easy traffic), backpressure and queue limits under load.

08Evaluation & monitoring

Offline eval before ship, online metrics after — drift detection, quality regression alerts, A/B testing, rollback strategy. In 2026 loops, this step gets as much airtime as the architecture itself.

09Iterate

What would you change with more time or scale? Naming this proactively signals seniority even when time runs out before you get there.

Adapted from the widely-used 9-step ML system design framework (see alirezadir's "Machine-Learning-Interviews" repo, §8) with the modern knowledge-strategy layer added for LLM-era questions.

The four most common failure modes

1) Jumping straight to a diagram before clarifying requirements. 2) Treating the model as a black box instead of discussing latency budgets, versioning, and rollback. 3) Picking RAG or fine-tuning by reflex instead of justifying the choice against the requirements. 4) Never mentioning monitoring at all — several interviewers on record say this alone is enough to sink an otherwise strong answer.

§6 — Interview Preparation

Five dimensions, not one

Standard advice covers maybe 40% of what an AI-engineer loop actually tests. It's five overlapping dimensions, and skipping any one of them is a common way strong candidates still get a no-hire.

01SWE coding

1–2 problems, 40–45 minutes. Data structures, algorithms, tree/graph traversal. Not the differentiator, but a hard gate — a miss here often means instant rejection regardless of everything else.

02"AI coding"

Implement k-means, attention, or batch norm from scratch — NumPy only, no PyTorch. This is the round most candidates freeze on because they prepared for standard SWE coding and nothing else. Phase 2 and Week 22 of the roadmap exist specifically for this.

03System design

Usually the highest-weighted round at senior level, and where §5's framework pays off. Increasingly includes an evaluation/monitoring sub-round distinct from architecture.

04Domain deep-dive

For AI/LLM-focused roles: conversational AI, RAG design, agent architecture. Shallow answers read as "hasn't actually built this" — your flagship projects are the antidote.

05Behavioral

Company-specific values, not generic STAR. A single weak behavioral round can undo four hours of strong technical performance — treat it with equal prep weight.

§9 — Reality Check

What this plan can promise, and what it can't

What's realistic

  • Multiple independent sources converge on the same number: someone who already knows Python, SQL, statistics, and general software-engineering fundamentals can reach interview-ready AI-engineering competence in 6–9 months of focused work. You start ahead of "complete beginner" on exactly these axes.
  • At ~30 hrs/week for 26 weeks, you're logging roughly 780 focused hours — more than most bootcamps, with five deployed, evaluated, monitored systems to show for it, not a certificate.
  • $140K+ is not an aggressive target against the data in §2 — it's close to the market floor for AI/ML engineering roles at funded companies, before you even reach the frontier-lab tier.

What's genuinely hard right now

  • Entry-level hiring at the largest, most famous companies has tightened sharply since 2022 by multiple independent measures — a "guaranteed" offer at one specific famous logo in 6 months is not a promise anyone can honestly make, from a roadmap or otherwise.
  • Neither "AI Engineer" nor "ML Engineer" postings are especially entry-friendly as titles — single-digit percentages of postings are explicitly entry-level. This is a real headwind, not a reason to stop.
  • Outcomes depend on variables this document can't control: interview-day performance, timing, geography and visa status, and plain luck in who reads your application when.

The one finding worth remembering above all the others

Across every hiring-side source researched for this document, the same conclusion repeats in different words: the market has split into people who treat AI/ML as coursework and people who ship production systems, and only the second group is clearing the strong offers. That's the entire logic behind this roadmap's shape — five real, evaluated, deployed, monitored projects; a phase explicitly on production engineering; and a system-design section built around "what happens after you ship," not just "what happens on stage one." Follow it honestly and you will be a genuinely strong candidate. Whether that becomes an offer at Google specifically, or at Databricks, or at a well-funded startup that outpays several famous names — that part, no document can guarantee. Build the range of options in §7, not a single bet.
